Malware Roundup: 5 Most Active Malware in the Wild

Lumma Stealer Analysis:

Based on the currently available data, Lumma Stealer is an information stealer malware that has been available through a malware-as-a-service (MaaS) model on Russian-speaking forums since at least August 2022. The malware primarily targets cryptocurrency wallets and two-factor authentication (2FA) browser extensions, before ultimately stealing sensitive information from the victim's machine. Threat actor "Shamel" is believed to be the developer of Lumma Stealer, who goes by the alias "Lumma". The malware features a non-resident loader that can deliver additional payloads via EXE, DLL, and PowerShell files. TTPs mentioned include HTTP POST requests using the user agent "teslabrowser/5.5" for exfiltration to a C2 server. Lumma Stealer is likely used by threat actors such as "Shamel's" group, including "Andariel", "Byzantine", and "Candor Group 3". The malware's use of MaaS platforms and its targeting of cryptocurrency wallets suggest that it may be part of a larger campaign to steal sensitive financial information.

Cobalt Strike Analysis:

Based on the currently available data, Cobalt Strike is a sophisticated malware toolkit that allows attackers to deploy a range of malicious payloads, including command execution, keylogging, and lateral movement tools. The malware's in-memory/file-less design makes it difficult to detect, and its support for C2 and staging over HTTP, HTTPS, DNS, SMB, and TCP protocols enables it to maintain persistence on compromised systems. Threat actors associated with Cobalt Strike include groups such as Wincobalt Strike, Mango, Tangelo, and others, which have been linked to high-profile attacks in various regions. These groups often employ advanced tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs) such as using Cobalt Strike to conduct reconnaissance, create backdoors, and exfiltrate sensitive data. The malware's customization capabilities also enable attackers to tailor their campaigns to specific targets and objectives. Notably, the use of Cobalt Strike by threat actors suggests a level of sophistication and resources that is often associated with state-sponsored or highly organized cybercrime groups. Overall, Cobalt Strike remains a significant threat in the cybersecurity landscape, requiring continued monitoring and analysis to stay ahead of its evolving TTPs.

The use of various encryption methods and obfuscation techniques suggests that the malware is highly sophisticated and difficult to detect. Overall, the data suggests that AsyncRat is a significant threat actor with a range of malicious capabilities.
